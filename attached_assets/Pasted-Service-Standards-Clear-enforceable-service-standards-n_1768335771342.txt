Service Standards

Clear, enforceable service standards—not vague hospitality language.
Guest experience is a designed system that protects consistency, speed, and margins.

Principle
Core Principle: Consistency Is the Product

Service standards exist to protect consistency, not to create robots.

Every guest should receive the same quality experience:

regardless of which server they get

regardless of shift, volume, or day of week

Hospitality without structure feels good once.
Hospitality with structure builds a business.

Framework
The Four-Point Greeting (MANDATORY)

Every table receives the same opening sequence:

Immediate Acknowledgment

Eye contact within 10 seconds

Verbal acknowledgment within 60 seconds

Warm Introduction

Server name stated

Tone confident, not scripted

Expectation Setting

“I’ll be right back with drinks” or

“Can I start you with something to drink while you look things over?”

Menu Confidence Cue

One relevant suggestion (not a full sales pitch)

If the greeting is rushed or skipped, the entire service feels broken—even if food is perfect.

Checklist
Server Timing Standards (Non-Negotiable)

TIMING IS A STANDARD, NOT A SUGGESTION

□ Greet table within 60 seconds of seating
□ Drink order taken within 2 minutes
□ Drinks delivered within 4 minutes
□ Food order taken within 8 minutes (if guests are ready)
□ Check-back within 2 bites or 2 minutes
□ Mid-meal table touch before plates are half finished
□ Check presented within 3 minutes of dessert decline or final plate clear
□ Table reset within 5 minutes of departure

Timing failures are service failures—even if guests don’t complain.

Framework
Table Touch Cadence (Service Control System)

Servers are expected to touch tables at intentional moments, not randomly.

REQUIRED TOUCH POINTS

Post-drink delivery

Two-bite / two-minute check

Mid-meal quality check

Pre-dessert / final clear

Farewell & invitation back

Managers observe cadence, not just friendliness.

Script
Guest Recovery Decision Tree (ROAR / HEAT Aligned)

When something goes wrong, recovery follows a structured escalation, not improvisation.

STEP 1 — ACKNOWLEDGE (Immediately)

“I see the issue, and I’m handling it right now.”

STEP 2 — APOLOGIZE (Without Excuses)

“I’m sorry this happened.”
No explanations. No blame. No defensiveness.

STEP 3 — ACT (Within Authority Limits)

Common Scenarios:

Wrong item: Replace + offer appetizer or dessert

Long wait (15+ minutes over quote): Complimentary round or dessert

Cold food: Replace + comp item

Poor staff attitude: Manager visit + meaningful comp

STEP 4 — ASSURE

“I want to make sure you leave happy.”

STEP 5 — FOLLOW-THROUGH

Manager check before departure, not after the complaint is over.

Recovery without follow-up feels transactional.
Recovery with follow-up rebuilds trust.

Framework
Comp Authority Limits (Margin Protection Built In)

Authority must be clear or comps become emotional.

SERVER
• Free dessert
• Free non-alcoholic drink
• Item replacement

BARTENDER
• One round comped
• Free appetizer

SHIFT LEAD
• Up to $25 in comps without approval

MANAGER
• Up to $100
• Full meal if warranted

OVER $100
• Owner notification required (approval can be retroactive)

Comps are tools, not apologies.
Use them intentionally.

Enforcement Standard (What Managers Actually Check)

Managers are responsible for:

Timing adherence

Greeting quality

Table touch cadence

Recovery execution

Comp justification

Servers are coached on patterns, not one-off mistakes.

Consultant’s Bottom Line

Great service is not personality-dependent.
It is system-dependent.

If your best service only happens when your best server is on the floor, you don’t have standards—you have luck.

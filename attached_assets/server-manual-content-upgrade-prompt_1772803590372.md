### DAY 2: MENU KNOWLEDGE (FOOD FOCUS) — Content Gaps

**Current gaps vs. industry standard:**

1. **No allergen protocol — only allergen awareness.** Industry standard (and Texas DSHS guideline) is that servers must know the *response protocol*, not just that allergens exist. 90% of allergen incidents happen because the server didn't communicate the order clearly to the kitchen.

Add:
```
Allergen Response Protocol:
1. Guest declares allergy → repeat it back immediately: 
   "I want to make sure I have that right — you have a [allergy] allergy?"
2. Note it on the ticket in POS under special instructions — every time.
3. Tell the expo directly when placing the order. Do not assume POS is enough.
4. Check with kitchen before bringing the dish: 
   "This is for a [allergy] guest — confirm this is safe?"
5. Deliver personally. Never have a food runner deliver an allergy plate 
   without briefing them first.
6. If there is any doubt → get the manager. This is not negotiable.

Common hidden allergens at Mouton's:
• Gluten: roux, flour-based sauces, fried items (shared fryer)
• Shellfish: Shrimp & Grits, any gumbo base
• Dairy: white gravy (Chicken Fried Steak), cream-based dishes
• Tree nuts: verify with kitchen on specials
```

2. **No table-side description coaching.** Industry standard: servers should be trained in sensory language, not just ingredient lists.

Add:
```
How to Describe Dishes:
Don't recite ingredients. Sell the experience.

Instead of: "It's shrimp with grits and a Cajun sauce."
Say: "It's our most popular dish — Gulf shrimp over stone-ground grits 
with a rich Cajun cream sauce. The kind of dish people come back for."

Three-part description: [Main protein/feature] + [how it's prepared] + 
[the experience or signature element]

Practice your top 5 dishes before Day 4. You'll be asked to describe them 
to your trainer as if they're a first-time guest.
```

3. **Dietary substitutions not mapped.** Operators lose covers when servers can't answer dietary questions confidently.

Add:
```
Dietary Modifications (confirmed with kitchen — verify any changes):
• Gluten-sensitive: Jambalaya Pasta → basmati rice substitution
• Dairy-free: confirm sauce bases with kitchen
• Vegetarian options: [list from Setup — Signature Dishes field]
• "Can we do that without X?" → always say "Let me check for you" 
  rather than yes or no without confirming

Never guess on dietary restrictions. Check. Every time.
```

Add to KEY POINTS chips: **"Allergen response protocol"**, **"Sensory description training"**, **"Never guess on allergies"**

### DAY 3: BEVERAGE MENU KNOWLEDGE — Content Gaps

**Current gaps vs. industry standard:**

1. **No beverage upsell system.** Beverages are the highest-margin item on any table. Most servers leave money on the table because they were never taught a structured upsell sequence.

Add:
```
The Beverage Sequence:
1. Greet and offer a specific cocktail: 
   "Can I start you with one of our Bayou Mules or something from the bar?"
   (Never ask "Can I get you something to drink?" — it's a yes/no question.)
2. If they order water or unsweetened tea → follow up once: 
   "Absolutely. We also have [house cocktail or wine] if you'd like something 
   to start. Can I tell you about our Happy Hour specials?"
3. With the menu: mention 1 cocktail, 1 wine, 1 beer relevant to 
   what they ordered or the time of day
4. Check back after first sip: "How's that drink treating you?" 
   This opens a second round conversation naturally.

Beverage attachment rate goal: every table should have at least one 
beverage beyond water. Track your own average.
```

2. **No responsible service framework beyond TABC compliance.** Industry standard is a proactive slow-down protocol.

Add:
```
Responsible Service — Beyond Compliance:
TABC says what you can't do. This is what you should do.

Signs to watch for:
• Slurred speech, impaired balance, unusually loud behavior
• Ordering rounds faster than they're consuming
• Guest appearing confused or disoriented

What to do:
1. Slow down the round without drawing attention: 
   "Let me get that order in for you in just a minute."
2. Offer water and food proactively: 
   "Can I bring out some of our Crawfish Dip while you wait?"
3. Inform your shift manager quietly — never make it a public confrontation
4. If manager approves cut-off: "I appreciate you all tonight — 
   I'm going to close out your tab and get you some water."
5. Document the interaction on your closing report

Protecting the guest protects you, the restaurant, and your TABC certification.
```

3. **No House Wine / Beer program knowledge.** 

Add:
```
Wine Service Basics:
• Present the bottle label to the guest who ordered before opening
• Pour a small taste for the person who ordered — wait for approval
• Pour ladies first, then clockwise around the table
• Never fill a wine glass more than 1/3 full
• Offer a second glass before the first is empty

Beer Service:
• Offer a glass for bottled beer — don't assume
• Know your draft list cold: [drafts from Setup field]
• Describe the draft with one word: "That's our lighter option" 
  or "That's our richest — full and malty"
```

Add to KEY POINTS chips: **"Beverage upsell sequence"**, **"Responsible service protocol"**, **"Wine service steps"**

### DAY 4: SERVICE STEPS — BASICS — Content Gaps

**Current content is strong. Fill these three gaps:**

1. **No ticket accuracy system.** Ticket errors are the #1 cause of kitchen conflict, remakes, and food cost spikes.

Add:
```
Ticket Accuracy:
• Read the order back before leaving the table. Every table. Every time.
• Special requests go in POS notes — not in your memory.
• Modifier codes you must know cold: [pull from POS system in Setup]
  - Gluten-sensitive flag
  - No [allergen] 
  - Temperature (steak temps: Rare / Medium Rare / Medium / Medium Well / Well)
  - On the side
  - Extra / light
• A wrong ticket costs the kitchen 15 minutes and costs you a tip.
  Own the ticket. Own the outcome.
```

2. **No "handling the wait" protocol.** One of the most common service failures.

Add:
```
Managing the Wait:
If a table's food is taking longer than expected (over 12 minutes for 
appetizers, 20 minutes for entrées during normal volume):

1. Touch the table before they ask: 
   "I wanted to check in — your food is being prepared right now. 
   Can I freshen your drinks while you wait?"
2. Get an honest ETA from expo — don't guess
3. If it's going to be significantly delayed: alert your manager immediately
4. Never say "I don't know" and walk away. 
   "I'm going to find out right now" is always the answer.

A table that feels taken care of during a wait tips better than a table 
with perfect fast service that felt ignored.
```

3. **No payment handling protocol.** Gap in almost every training manual.

Add:
```
Payment & Check Handling:
• Drop the check presenter face-down, never announce the total
• "No rush — whenever you're ready" after placing the check
• If paying by card: process within 3 minutes of receiving it
• If paying cash: return change promptly — never assume it's all a tip
• Split checks: set up in POS before the meal ends, not at checkout
  "Would you like me to split that check for you tonight?" — ask early
• Never hover after dropping the check. Return in a natural window.
• Thank them by name if you saw the card: 
  "Thank you, Mr. Johnson — we hope to see you again."
```

Add to KEY POINTS chips: **"Ticket accuracy"**, **"Managing the wait"**, **"Payment protocol"**

### DAY 6: ADVANCED GUEST EXPERIENCE — Content Gaps

**Current HEAT method is excellent. Add these three missing areas:**

1. **No large party / event service protocol.** One of the highest failure points in any restaurant.

Add:
```
Large Party Service (6+ guests):
• Pre-bus constantly — tables fill up faster with larger parties
• Drink orders: take the whole table at once, repeat back, 
  confirm modifications before leaving
• Food orders: number the seats before ordering (seat #1 = closest to 
  door or designated anchor). Never ask "who had the salmon?"
• Verify split check preference before ordering — not at payment
• Communication with kitchen: large party tickets get flagged in POS. 
  Alert expo when you drop the ticket so they can pace the fire.
• Tip on large parties: auto-gratuity policy is [set in Setup]. 
  If applied, inform the table gracefully: 
  "Just so you know, an 18% gratuity is included for parties of 6 or more."
```

2. **No regular guest / loyalty recognition system.** Independent restaurants win on this — it's their primary advantage over chains.

Add:
```
Recognizing Regulars:
The single biggest advantage an independent restaurant has over a chain 
is that we can actually remember people.

• Learn names. Use them: "Welcome back, Mr. Thompson."
• Remember preferences when possible: "The Shrimp & Grits again, or 
  trying something new tonight?"
• Note specials that align with what they love
• If a regular brings new guests: acknowledge it — 
  "We're glad you brought friends in tonight."
• Flag regulars to your manager — loyalty deserves recognition at the 
  management level too

You are not just taking orders. You are building relationships that bring 
people back every Friday night for the next ten years.
```

3. **No service recovery standard beyond HEAT.** HEAT handles complaints. This covers the step after.

Add:
```
After Service Recovery:
HEAT resolves the immediate issue. This is what you do next.

1. Return to the table within 5 minutes: 
   "I just wanted to check back — is everything better?"
2. If manager comped an item: you don't need to apologize again. 
   Acknowledge it once: "I appreciate your patience tonight."
3. Close the interaction on a positive note — they should leave feeling 
   taken care of, not like they caused a problem
4. Document what happened on your closing report so management has context
5. If the guest says they won't return: thank them for telling you and 
   let the manager know immediately

A guest who had a problem handled well is more loyal than one who had 
a perfect visit. Own the recovery.
```

Add to KEY POINTS chips: **"Large party protocol"**, **"Regular guest recognition"**, **"Service recovery follow-through"**

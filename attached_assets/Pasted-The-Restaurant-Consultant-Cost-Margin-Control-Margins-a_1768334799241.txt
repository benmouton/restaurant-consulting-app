Cost & Margin Control

Margins are protected before ordering—not after inventory shock.

Core Principle

Food cost is decided before the order is placed, not after the inventory is counted.

If you are shocked by your food cost, the failure did not happen in accounting.
It happened during prep, portioning, plating, and menu design.

Food cost is a design outcome, not a surprise.

Framework: How to Calculate Food Cost (NON-NEGOTIABLE)
1. Plate Cost Method (Primary Control Method)

Every menu item must have a known plate cost.

Formula:

Ingredient Cost ÷ Yield = Usable Cost  
Usable Cost × Portion Size = Plate Cost


Example:

Ribeye: $12.00 / lb

Yield after trim: 80% → usable cost = $15.00 / lb

Portion: 10 oz (0.625 lb)

Plate cost = $9.38

Rule:
If you cannot state a plate cost within ±$0.25, you do not control food cost.

2. Target Food Cost Method (Menu Design Control)

Food cost targets are set before menu approval.

Formula:

Plate Cost ÷ Target Food Cost % = Menu Price


Example:

Plate cost: $6.00

Target food cost: 28%

Required price: $21.43 (round UP, never down)

Rule:
Never price emotionally. Price mathematically.

3. The 1-Ounce Test (Line Reality Check)

Every protein must pass the 1-oz deviation test.

Formula:

Cost per ounce × Extra ounces × Covers = Margin Loss


Example:

Protein cost: $0.90 / oz

Over-portion: +2 oz

Covers per shift: 100

Loss = $180 per shift

Rule:
If cooks don’t understand this math, you haven’t trained them.

Checklist: Portion Control Enforcement
DAILY ENFORCEMENT

□ Digital scales at every protein station
□ Portion guides posted with photos and weights
□ Manager spot-checks 3 plates per service
□ Portion compliance included in line checks
□ Expo authorized to reject plates off spec

WHEN OVER-PORTIONING OCCURS

Show the spec vs. what was plated

State the cost difference clearly

Multiply by covers (make it real)

Correct immediately

Document if pattern continues

Reminder: Over-portioning is not generosity.
It is theft of margin and must be treated seriously.

Framework: Waste Log That Actually Works
WHAT TO TRACK (MANDATORY)

• Date
• Item
• Quantity
• Reason (spoiled / dropped / returned / overcooked / overprepped)
• Who logged it
• Assigned cost (weekly, not optional)

WEEKLY WASTE REVIEW (15 MINUTES, NO EXCUSES)

• Total waste cost
• Waste % of food cost
• Top 3 waste items
• Root cause for each
• Corrective action assigned with owner

Benchmark:

Under 2% → acceptable

2–3% → warning

Over 3% → immediate investigation

Waste without review is permission to repeat.

Framework: Menu Complexity Warning Signs
YOUR MENU IS TOO COMPLEX IF:

• Ingredients appear in only one dish
• Daily prep list exceeds 30 items
• Line cooks need references during service
• Food cost fluctuates more than 3% month-to-month
• Ticket times regularly exceed standards

SIMPLIFICATION FRAMEWORK

Cross-utilize proteins (2–3 dishes minimum)

Limit sauces (5–7 total)

Standardize base preparations

one mashed potato

one rice

one stock

Remove the lowest-selling 10% of items quarterly

Complexity hides waste. Simplicity exposes it.

Verification Loop (THIS IS WHAT MOST OPERATORS MISS)

Every cost control system must include:
• Standard (spec, recipe, portion)
• Training (shown, not explained)
• Verification (daily checks)
• Correction (real-time)
• Documentation (patterns, not one-offs)

If any step is missing, the system is broken.

Consultant’s Bottom Line

You do not “watch” food cost.
You design it, train it, enforce it, and verify it.

If margins are thin, the solution is almost never:

higher prices

better vendors

more accounting

It is tighter systems.

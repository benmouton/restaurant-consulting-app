Crisis Management
Control the Moment. Protect the System.

Restaurants don’t avoid crises.
They survive them — or they don’t.

The difference between a bad night and a business-ending event is simple:
Do you have a playbook, or are you improvising in public?

Crisis plans exist so decisions are made before emotions take over.

Core Principle: Calm Is a System

Chaos spreads when leadership hesitates.

In any crisis:

One person takes control

One plan is followed

One message is delivered

If everyone is deciding, no one is leading.

Service Meltdown Playbook

(Used When the Floor or Kitchen Is Losing Control)

Trigger Conditions

Initiate this playbook when two or more are true:

Ticket times exceeding standards

Expo losing sequence

Guest complaints escalating

Kitchen visibly underwater

Staff arguing or freezing

STEP 1: RECOGNIZE

Say it out loud.

“We’re behind. We’re switching to recovery mode.”

Naming the problem stops denial and activates leadership.

STEP 2: COMMAND

One manager takes the window. No exceptions.

“I’m running expo. Nothing plates without my call.”

All decisions route through expo

No freelancing

No side conversations

Control reduces panic.

STEP 3: CONTAIN

Protect future guests before fixing past mistakes.

“Host, we’re on a 15-minute wait even if tables are open.”

Pause seating if needed

Combine or close sections

Reduce menu complexity if required

You cannot recover while expanding the problem.

STEP 4: COMMUNICATE

Silence turns delay into anger.

Every waiting table gets:

A real update

A human touch

Ownership from leadership

“We hit an unexpected rush. I’m personally tracking your order.”

Never blame the kitchen.
Never blame staff.
Take responsibility.

STEP 5: RECOVER

Fix the relationship, not just the check.

Comp strategically (not emotionally)

Touch every affected table

Thank guests for patience

The goal is trust restored, not money saved.

STEP 6: DEBRIEF (MANDATORY)

After service — not during — document:

What broke?

Why did it break?

What system failed?

What change prevents this next time?

If it’s not documented, it will happen again.

Staff Walkout Protocol

(When Someone Leaves Mid-Shift)

This is disruptive — not catastrophic — if handled correctly.

Immediate Response

Do not chase.
Let them go. Escalation makes it worse.

Assess coverage.
Can remaining staff absorb sections?

Stabilize the floor.

Combine sections

Close areas if necessary

Call backup only if it helps, not panics

Communicate clearly to staff.

“[Name] left. Here’s how we’re adjusting.”

No gossip. No speculation.

Acknowledge effort.
Thank staff during service and again after.

After the Shift

Document the incident immediately

Process termination paperwork next business day

Maintain professionalism at all times

Never badmouth the employee.
Leadership tone becomes culture.

Prevention Systems

Regular check-ins with at-risk employees

Conflict addressed early, not emotionally

Exit interviews that ask:

“What would have kept you?”

Walkouts are usually system failures, not surprises.

Owner Burnout Recovery Protocol

(This Is an Operational Risk, Not a Personal Weakness)

Burned-out owners make bad decisions, kill morale, and stall growth.

Warning Signs

If three or more are true, you are in danger:

□ Dreading being in the restaurant
□ Snapping over small issues
□ Avoiding delegation
□ Constant exhaustion
□ Neglecting personal relationships
□ Feeling indispensable
□ Resentment toward staff or guests

Mandatory Recovery Actions

□ Take two consecutive days off — fully offline
□ Identify three tasks only you must do
□ List ten tasks you’re doing that someone else could
□ Delegate two tasks this week
□ Schedule weekly off-site thinking time
□ Maintain at least one owner-level peer conversation monthly

Burnout doesn’t resolve itself.
It compounds.

The Hard Truth

If your restaurant can’t survive 48 hours without you,
you don’t own a business — you own a job with overhead.

Crisis planning exists to:

Protect guests

Protect staff

Protect the owner

In that order.

The Standard

Crises are inevitable.
Public panic is optional.

Prepared restaurants recover faster,
learn more,
and earn trust when it matters most.
